This year I crossed a border most career advice pretends isn't there. Same person, same skills, two companies with opposite physics: a marketplace at scale, where a wrong bold move costs more than a hundred cautious-but-slow ones, and its earlier-stage international spin-off, where the only fatal move is not moving. The behaviours that read as "senior leadership" on one side read as liabilities on the other — and nothing about me changed. Only the loss function did.

There is no good leader in the abstract. There is a mode that fits the company you're in, and — the part the org chart doesn't show — a route through your own career that trades one kind of value for another, with a valley in the middle that swallows good people. This is a guide to reading both: the company's loss function, and the craft-versus-leverage curve you're actually choosing your spot on.

## The company picks the loss function

Before you pick a style, read what the organisation is optimising — it isn't taste, it's a cost structure. An incumbent platform at scale optimises for coordination and control: a wrong bold move across a hundred million users is the expensive error, slow is cheap and mostly invisible, so the load-bearing skills are alignment, predictability, and process. A startup or spin-off at launch optimises for speed and learning: *not* moving is the fatal error, a wrong move is cheap and reversible, so the load-bearing skills are thin command, founder context, and deciding on partial data. Same behaviour — "surface every risk loudly and align before acting" — is diligence in the first world and drag in the second.

That's the first axis — the world you're in. The second is the one you choose inside it, and it's the one that decides your ceiling.

## The axis under founder mode and manager mode

Paul Graham's "Founder Mode" (2024) named the split everyone now argues over. **Manager mode** scales through delegation and clean interfaces: hire well, hand over ownership, stay out of the details. **Founder mode** reaches into the details on the bets that matter, keeps context in one head, earns authority through competence rather than title. But "founder vs manager" is the surface. The axis underneath is **craft versus leverage**: craft is value you produce *yourself*; leverage is value you produce *through other people*. Every promotion past a point trades one for the other, and the leadership-development ladder (Joiner and Josephs, *Leadership Agility*, 2006) is really a map of that trade — Expert (solve it yourself) → Achiever (orchestrate outcomes) → Catalyst (build the org's capacity). Each rung asks you to do less of the thing that got you the last one.

The trap is imagining the trade is linear — that you climb smoothly from craft to leverage, gaining altitude the whole way. You don't. The curve has a valley.

## The valley

Plot value against how far you've moved from doing toward managing, and you don't get a ramp — you get two peaks with a trough between them.

Call it the **craft–leverage valley**, and the first-line manager stands at the bottom of it — dangerous precisely because it looks like progress. You've been given a small team and a bet or two, so it reads as a promotion. But you've stopped producing (the craft goes stale the moment you stop using it), and you don't yet allocate anything worth calling leverage: a bet or two is not a portfolio, it's a rounding error on the org's capacity. You've paid the full price of leaving the craft — and bought almost none of the leverage. The week tells the story before the title does: one-on-ones, a calibration doc, a hiring loop, a status deck — and the roadmap you used to write now has someone else's name on it. The people who get stuck here took the step for the title, or to "stop grinding on the work," and discovered the valley has no view.

The obvious objection: for most people the valley is the only bridge — almost nobody jumps from senior IC to head-of. True, and it sharpens the advice instead of defeating it: the valley is a transit, not a destination, and the fix is to cross it like one. Enter only when real scope is contracted on the other side — a portfolio, not a promise. Keep one owned bet alive as craft insurance while you cross. And set a clock: if two performance cycles pass and the scope hasn't widened, you're no longer crossing, you're camping. The other exit is just as legitimate — back to the craft end as a genuine principal. The only losing move is to drift in and stay.

One stitch back to the first axis: the company's loss function sets the relative height of the two peaks. At a launch-phase company the craft peak towers — founder context is the scarce asset; at a scaled incumbent the org-builder peak does — coordination is. The valley, though, is there in every cell.

## Founder mode has a width

The craft end isn't a single point — it has a width, and the width is what separates a senior specialist from a principal. Founder mode can run over a *narrow, deep* surface (a comms or CRM product you hold entirely in your head) or a *broad* one (a country P&L, an assortment strategy, a whole tech org). The rare and expensive leaders hold founder mode over a *wide* surface — deciding fast, in the details, across a domain most people could only manage by delegating it away. The constraint is span — the handful of live threads one head can genuinely hold, five to nine on a good week: you cannot be the best individual mind on every problem across a P&L, so widening forces a depth trade — you keep founder-mode *judgment* across the surface while surrendering founder-mode *hands* on most of it. That's the real principal skill, and it's why "principal IC" and "senior engineer" are different jobs, not different levels of the same one. Sport has the right name for the figure at this end: the **playing coach** — still on the field on the hardest problem, leading a small team by example rather than by org chart.

## Two currencies of leverage — and why the IC route is no escape

Here's the distinction that kills the most common misconception about the two tracks. Leverage comes in two currencies. **Granted leverage** is what the manager gets: direct resources — a team they can point at a problem by authority. **Earned leverage** is what the principal IC gets: nothing granted, so everything moves by influence without authority — bargaining for other people's time, building the coalition, being right persuasively enough that teams you don't own choose to help.

An MBA case I never forgot put it cleanly: the engineer at a big company and the program manager beside them. The engineer has resources and autonomy — they can just *build*. The program manager has an ambitious goal and no resources at all, and has to hustle, influence, and bargain for every hour of someone else's team. Responsibility without authority isn't a manager's burden; at the extreme it's the IC-influencer's entire operating condition.

Which is why the IC route is *not* the escape from people and politics it's sold as. You still read the room, still hear the half-tones — the unstated objection, the "yes" that means "no," whose real concern is hiding behind the stated one — and you do it with *less* positional cover than a manager, because you can't fall back on authority. But — and this is the honest calibration — the principal IC is still **building-weighted**: more protected maker time, and the politics is a *tax* on the build, not the substance of the job. The head or lead is **people-weighted**: managing the team, developing people, calibration and stakeholder work *is* the role, and building time trends to zero. So the trade isn't "craft = no people, leadership = people." It's how much of your week is protected for building versus spent on the people machinery — with relational skill required at *every* point on the curve, and the valley being exactly where you've taken on the people cost without either the craft or the leverage to justify it.

## Why the curve is tilting now

None of this is static — AI and org design are re-pricing the whole curve toward the building end. AI raises the ceiling on what one person can produce, so a unit of craft is worth more per head than it was: the deep-IC peak is rising. And organisations are flattening — "member of technical staff" as a title, fewer layers, senior people expected to stay hands-on — which thins the manager scaffolding that used to be the *only* route up. The dual-track ladders big tech formalised already concede the point — top IC bands overlap manager bands — and the frontier labs push past conceding it: flat, IC-heavy, founder-mode by default. The practical consequence is that the valley is getting deeper (there's less reason to pass through first-line management to prove yourself) and the old advice — that the manager track is the only summit — is aging in real time. It's now genuinely possible to build a top-of-curve career without ever leaving the craft end. That was not true a decade ago.

## Running both, and reading the person

Whichever end you hold — the widened principal, or the head who kept a bet — the working discipline is the same, and it's also what makes the valley crossing survivable: run **founder mode on the one or two bets you genuinely own** and **manager mode on everything else**, with a boundary held on purpose. The failure modes are both bleeds across it: founder instinct leaking into a peer's backlog (now you're micromanaging a team that isn't yours), or manager dilution leaking onto the bet only you could carry (now it's stuck in consensus). The tell is concrete — if you can't name your one or two owned bets in a sentence, the boundary has dissolved; if you're equally deep in six things, you're running founder mode past its span.

The same discipline points at people, not just bets. The productive mode flexes with the person's maturity on the task — direct the novice, delegate to the master, and never apply one default to both (Hersey and Blanchard called it situational leadership; the delegation literature, Oncken and Wass's 1974 "Who's Got the Monkey?", frames it as moving each person up a ladder of initiative rather than taking the work back because you'd finish it faster). Founder-mode operators default to "I'll just do it," which is exactly the reflex that caps their team at their own throughput. The whole craft is refusing to have a default.

## What assessments can — and can't — see

Companies rightly try to place the right type of person in the right role, and a structured leadership assessment is a real tool for it: it measures *fit to an archetype*, which is decision-grade signal about a *match*. The failure is reading it as a measure of leadership *ability*, because the thing it can't see is judgment. A rule is scoreable; judgment reads as deviation. "Always escalate a discrepancy loudly" scores well on transparency; the senior move — first ask *by how much are we off*, because a 0.3% drift is a Slack thread and a 30% drop is a war room — produces the same observable "didn't escalate on sight" as negligence would, so the instrument scores the triage as a gap. It's Goodhart's Law with an HR lanyard: the legible behaviour becomes the target, and the illegible-but-correct one reads as a deficiency.

And the placement these systems most often get wrong is the valley itself: promoting a high-craft IC into first-line management because "leadership means managing people," when the person's whole value was the craft they've now been told to set down. The honest caveat cuts both ways — some flagged behaviours are worth fixing anywhere (burying bad news is worse leadership in every world; "build teams that don't need you in the room" is genuinely universal). Read your own scorecard as a compass toward an archetype, not a verdict on your worth — and keep the parts that are true regardless of which cell you're standing in.

## Pick your spot on purpose

Three readers, three jobs. **If you're choosing a route:** run the diagnosis in order — *what loss function does my company optimise* (control or speed), *am I building leverage through craft or through people*, and *which end of the curve am I aiming at* — then move toward an end deliberately, and never mistake the first-line valley for the destination. **If you're a manager scoring an IC:** score situational reasoning, not archetype conformance — ask *why this call, why now, why proportional* — and you'll catch the judgment the instrument can't. **If you're designing the ladder:** stop making first-line management the only door out of the craft, or you'll keep marching your best builders into the valley and calling it promotion.

There is no good leader in the abstract. There's a mode that fits the company, a spot on the curve that fits you, and one discipline that travels across all of it: you are always choosing what to lose touch with. Choose it on purpose — and never trade your craft for a seat in the valley.
